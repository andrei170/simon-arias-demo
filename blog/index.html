What to Look For in a Motivational Speaker
for Your Insurance Agency

Published May 13, 2026 · Simon Arias · 11 min read

Every insurance agency owner I've talked to has the same booking story. They paid a generic motivational speaker who lit up the room for 45 minutes, the team felt great, the production numbers didn't move. Here's what to actually look for the next time you book a keynote for your agency.

The problem with generic motivation

Most insurance teams have heard the same speeches. Mountain climber stories. Football coach lessons. Three-time author talking about resilience. The room laughs in the right places, the audience claps, the producer who's struggling to write a single application that month walks out wondering why nothing about her actual job changed.

The miss is structural, not motivational. Generic motivational speakers move emotions. They rarely move specific producer behaviors. And in an insurance agency, the behaviors are the business. The number of dials made. The number of qualified appointments set. The follow-up cadence on the prospect who said "let me think about it." The recruiting conversations that decide whether your team is 20 producers or 50 producers six months from now.

The speaker who moves the agency forward is the one whose own story is the agency story. Below are the five criteria the agency owners who book the right speakers actually use.

The five criteria for the right insurance keynote speaker

01

Real insurance industry experience

Not finance, not real estate, not corporate sales. The product is different. The buyer is different. The compensation structure is different. Look for a speaker who has built or scaled an actual insurance agency, not one who has spoken at insurance conferences. The first speaks the language by lived experience. The second has learned the vocabulary.

02

A track record you can measure

Ask for production numbers. Average premium per producer, growth rate of the agency over a five-year window, recruiting and retention numbers, top-producer revenue. Speakers worth booking have the figures available. If they can't tell you the actual agency they built or what it produces, they haven't built one. They've spoken about other people's agencies.

03

Topics the team can apply Monday morning

A keynote that ends with "go out there and crush it" has zero half-life. A keynote that ends with three specific behavioral shifts the producer can execute on their next dial, their next appointment, their next recruiting conversation has weeks of compounding effect. Ask the speaker for their last three keynote outlines. If you can read them and identify the takeaway, the team will too.

04

References from agency owners, not corporate clients

An insurance team has a different rhythm than a corporate sales floor or a Fortune 500 conference. A speaker who's killed it at a Salesforce keynote may bomb in front of a room of commission-paid life agents. Ask for agency-owner references specifically. Call two of them. Ask "did the production numbers move in the quarter after the speech?" The honest answer separates speakers who entertain from speakers who shift outcomes.

05

Delivery that holds a commission-paid room

Insurance producers are not corporate employees. They live and die by their next deal. They've heard every pitch, every motivational hook, every story arc. The room is the toughest commercial-audience room in the country. The speaker has to be in the discomfort with them, not above it. The ones who land are the ones who've personally faced what the producer in the back row is facing this Wednesday afternoon. Everyone else gets a polite ovation and a forgettable Tuesday.

Why industry experience is the criterion most owners skip

Insurance is a discipline with three structural features that make it different from every other sales floor:

  • The product is invisible. You can't show the buyer a car or a software demo. You're selling a contract that pays out years or decades later, often only if the buyer is dead. That changes the close cycle, the trust requirements, the agent's emotional load.
  • The buyer is risk-averse by definition. The reason someone agrees to a 30-minute appointment is because they're already nervous about what happens if they don't have coverage. The speaker has to understand the producer's job of moving the buyer through fear, not just objections.
  • The team works on commission with no salary floor. A bad week is a bad paycheck. A bad month means the family conversation. A speaker who's never sat in the producer's chair on a Friday night looking at a slow week doesn't understand the audience.

A speaker who's built an agency knows all three. A speaker who's read books about insurance knows zero of them.
